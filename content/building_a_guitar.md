## Risks

This project involved several design decisions that required a lot of testing, experimentation, and iteration. One of my biggest concerns was my side walls. I decided to make them out of living hinge because I felt like it gave it its own flair. As I have progressed through my journey of becoming a Mechanical Engineer, this was one of the things I saw used in other projects, and something that was also done once before by a previous [Tufts student](https://www.carliesylvan.com/projects/travel-classical-guitar)- Carlie Sylvan. It was a method of fabrication I wanted to try out myself. I didn’t take into consideration until later though that the holes would cause the sound to leak, so it would cause my instrument to not be as loud. As shown above though, this issue was solved. The other risk I took was the dowel setup to glue the neck and the body together, and make sure it was aligned. I had to create a dowel alignment setup, but the risk was a small misalignment lead to a big offset at the opposite end of the guitar neck. I had to do many test cuts, imitating the setup I had in order to get it right. After a couple tests, I still hesitated to drill, but I ended up doing it, and the alignment ended up a lot better than I expected. 

## Next Steps

Now that I have a finished guitar that can play sound, there are still a few things I have yet to do in order to call it a finished project. For starters, I don’t know how to play the guitar so a goal of mine is to be able to play a song by the time the semester ends. Apart from that personal goal, I plan to do these things: (when I finish them, they will have a ✅)

- Fix string distance
    - The distance from the 6 strings is tilted to the left, and that is because I made notches on my bridge so that the strings would sit over the neck and fretboard. I need to make new spacings on them so that they are equal distance from each other.
- Finish it (polycrylic)
    - I have polycrylic which I plan to use on the full guitar so it can have a shine to it. I am going to sand the guitar to be smooth and apply multiple layers.
- Cut strings
    - Because I know I am going to remove the strings in order to finish the guitar and do other fixes on it, I left the original length of the strings just in case.
- Cut frets to length
    - I tried to make the fret metals a tad bit longer then the fretboard itself, but they are still sharp and I do not want to cut myself with them.
- Mark 3 5 7 frets
    - Because I don’t know how to play guitar and upon trying to learn chords, I realized that I am counting the frets which is making it more tedious to learn. A visual mark on these would make my learning experience better.
- Protect Rossette
    - Since the rossette was laser cut, and had really small pieces, they have been falling out and I needed to add a ring of protection to the back. I plan to find a glue combination so the rest of the details can also stay protected.
- Pick guard
    - I also bought a pick guard which I plan to add to the guitar, as well as lining the rosette with it so it can have that extra layer of protection.
- Bridge and Saddle
    - Because my saddle is raised, it is causing the space between my soundhole and bridge to start caving in slowly. This is due to a lever motion that the new taller saddle is making, and its going to eventually cave in my soundboard with the force of the strings. I need to either support that space, or drop the saddle….

## Reflections:

- What I learned
    - I learned a ton about wood working, design, and fabricating while on the go. One of the biggest things I learned is that the design world compared to the real world are two complete different beasts. I felt like I did so much improvisation because things did not always go as planned.
- What I was most proud of
    - I think overall, I was proud about everything in this process. Woodworking is something I have watched on YouTube all the time and never thought I was also capable of making something myself. If I were to go through the process of building a guitar again, I would fabricate everything from scratch- except the hardware. I think I am capable a second time around of bending wood, making a fretboard and the neck.
- What took a while (was tough)
    - I think what took the longest to do was getting started. The same way there is a writer’s block, I think I fell under a similar experience. There was so much I knew I needed to do, and so much designing and iterating that I was hesitant to start. Ultimately, I started with one thing, and it snowballed after that to get the final product- a playable guitar.
- Most spectacular failure
    - I think I have 2, and both included the bridge and strings. I researched the neck and body portion so much and worried about it being straight- hyperfocusing on this a bit- and realized that although it was straight, it was angled back too much. This caused the nut to be higher than the bridge, and it caused buzzing and the strings to rest on it, rather than over it. The spacing of my strings in comparison to the bridge- of a full size guitar- when mine is 1/2 - 3/4 of the size, meant the spacing of the strings wasn’t correct.
