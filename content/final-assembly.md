# Final Assembly

Created: March 25, 2026 11:56 AM

The first step after getting all the subparts done, was gluing the front soundboard to the body. I waited 24 hours for it to properly set, and I moved onto the next step. 

![D10E45EC-2F4E-49F6-B85F-0BCD1761B9A8_4_5005_c.jpeg](/notion-images/final-assembly/D10E45EC-2F4E-49F6-B85F-0BCD1761B9A8_4_5005_c.jpeg)

Now that I was assembling the guitar, it was when I realized I needed to soundproof my body. Sound is important to bounce off the inside to be able to project, and my body would lose sound because of the small holes of the living hinge, so I lined the inside with wood veneer. 

![C500B7B3-2D6E-46FF-8D2A-AF2278A4AA25_1_105_c.jpeg](/notion-images/final-assembly/C500B7B3-2D6E-46FF-8D2A-AF2278A4AA25_1_105_c.jpeg)

![18EAECD9-B56B-4F2E-B9D4-181BD2663AB2_1_105_c.jpeg](/notion-images/final-assembly/18EAECD9-B56B-4F2E-B9D4-181BD2663AB2_1_105_c.jpeg)

Although it didn’t take long to line it, I still wanted to wait some time to let the glue set because I knew that once I closed it off, it’ll be real hard to reach on the inside of the body to fix any lose pieces. 

After I confirmed this was good, I moved onto attaching the neck to the sidepieces. This is when I started hesitating and considered a lot of options, talked to a lot of people, and researched A TON. I knew that any mess ups here would be exponential due to the distance of the objects. I ultimately landed on a dowel setup and had to make a jig for it. 

![37C6DEE4-0B5A-4664-8BC2-DD0DD92AF56A_4_5005_c.jpeg](/notion-images/final-assembly/37C6DEE4-0B5A-4664-8BC2-DD0DD92AF56A_4_5005_c.jpeg)

Because of the abnormal shapes of the guitar neck and also body blocks, there wasn’t any place to attach onto, so I fabricated this to line up the dowels. I had to replicate my setup of my soundboard, so I used the spare wood that I cut the body out of to offset the distance of the sidewall and fretboard. I also needed to measure how deep the dowel had to be, on both the block and neck. I drilled into one hole, and stuck a longer dowel in it. Once this was in, I knew that as long as the holes lined up with this setup, the neck would be straight. 

The risk of using dowels was a small offset would have radiated as you went along the neck and it would have caused more problems, so I made sure that it was aligned. I did a lot of test drills to make sure it worked, and I got them in, not straight on the body itself, but correlating to each other was good enough. 

You can see on this picture a bit, the positioning of the holes.

![C500B7B3-2D6E-46FF-8D2A-AF2278A4AA25_1_105_c.jpeg](/notion-images/final-assembly/C500B7B3-2D6E-46FF-8D2A-AF2278A4AA25_1_105_c_1.jpeg)

I knew the guitar would sound good after that. Thats when I glued the neck and prepared all the final details. I put the tuners in, and also glued my bridge, and set my saddle and nut where they needed to be. This is when I realized that my saddle was lower than my nut, so it meant the strings would rest on the 10th, 11th, and 12th frets. I either needed to sand down the fretboard by a lot, or raise the saddle. I glued 2 saddles on top of each other, but placing 2 strings, I heard a snap, and realized the saddle would need more support. 

![346B1F23-33B0-4133-A344-CBFB01A46BF3_1_105_c.jpeg](/notion-images/final-assembly/ceca0e8a-92a8-4209-bc1c-b294ba386a12.png)

I fabricated a wooden piece to support the bridge and saddle, and glued it where my bridge was. I put a lot of effort to make it fit seamlessly with the bridge and still support the saddle, while also not interfering with the strings themselves.

1. **Frequency Domain Analysis (Combined Power Spectrum)**

The Combined Power Spectrum illustrates the resonant behavior of the E, D, and B strings, characterized by sharp, high-intensity fundamental peaks. The 1**94 Hz**, 2**93 Hz,** and **246 Hz** spikes confirm that the soundboard and **Torres fan bracing** are successfully projecting intended pitches. However, the presence of low-level broadband noise between 1 kHz and 5 kHz suggests remaining structural energy loss, likely due to the **3 mm spruce** soundboard caving under bridge torque.

1. **Time-Domain Analysis (Waveforms)**

The waveform plots demonstrate the attack and decay characteristics of the instrument. While the initial transient is clean—indicating that the **stacked saddle** has successfully cleared the strings from the **bowed neck**—the decay slope is notably steep. Sustain is limited to approximately 3–4 seconds across all strings, suggesting that the mechanical stress on the bridge is dampening vibrations before they can fully resonate through the mahogany body.

1. **Spectrographic Analysis (Harmonic Content)**

The spectrograms provide a visual map of harmonic integrity over time. The consistent horizontal banding confirms stable intonation, yet there is a visible "dropout" of upper partials. This lack of high-frequency sustain is a signature of **bridge rotation**, where the twisting of the soundboard "mutes" the overtones. Correcting the neck bow by **sanding the fretboard level** will allow for a lower saddle, reducing this dampening effect.
