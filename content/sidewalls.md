From these, I found the line pattern to give the most flexibility, and I was able to also mess with the sizing as well. Smaller cuts would have given a lot more bendiness, but it also meant the wood would not have been as strong. I ultimately chose the 1.5mm wide thickness.  

## Kerfing

The next step I took was to make the guitar kerfings, as people make these 2 around the same time.  When speaking to different people about it and how kerfing is usually difficult to make, they advised me to 3D print rings.

I laser cut 2 ring halfs per side, and realized that the 3mm wood I was using was not enough to support the 86mm long wall. I wound up stacking 2 ring halves to make them 6mm tall, and did 3 layers rather than just 2. 

![BBC18039-9668-42C7-9DBC-E95B9094703B_1_105_c.jpeg](/notion-images/sidewalls/BBC18039-9668-42C7-9DBC-E95B9094703B_1_105_c.jpeg)

This is also when I made my wood blocks, one for the front and one for the back. I also wanted to envision the dimensions of the guitar as well.

## Sides

![F30B11D0-90F2-454E-BD77-7F2B57039D5C_1_105_c.jpeg](/notion-images/sidewalls/F30B11D0-90F2-454E-BD77-7F2B57039D5C_1_105_c.jpeg)

![Screenshot 2026-03-09 at 11.08.08 PM.png](/notion-images/sidewalls/Screenshot_2026-03-09_at_11.08.08_PM.png)

Because the perimeter of the guitar was longer than the bed size of the laser cutter at Nolop, I had to split it up into 4 separate pieces, and did finger joints at the ends so they can join to each other. It took 1 hour to print the 4 pieces, and I was ready to glue my sides together.

This is what the final sidewall looked like. It was one of the most tedious processes because I needed to make sure the kerfing would hold the walls properly, and I needed to do multiple iterations on it. Another thing I did consider but chose to ignore was bending the wood would distort the lengths, so I opted to have the missing piece that didnt close at the top and printed infill pieces for that as well.
